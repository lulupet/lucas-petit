Create an account on Lichess
You can create an account here.
Why creating an online account at all?
Many people start playing chess with an application (against a computer) and actually, you can rise to a pretty serious level of chess by dpoing so but it can be a bit boring. When you play online, you can play against other people which makes it way more challenging, you can do tactics exercises, you can analyze your games, etc. Beside, you have an Elo rating that tells your level compared to other players so that your progression is measurable.
Why Lichess?
Many other websites exist. The most famous is Chess.com but here are reasons why I prefer Lichess:
  • Lichess is free and will always be (you can give them some money so that they can keep existing but their main interest is not profitability). On Chess.com, you have to pay to get access to all of the features.
  • There is no ad on Lichess, there are if you don't pay on Chess.com.
  • Lichess is developed by an open source community and Chess.com is a company.
  • Lichess is French and Chess.com is American.
Learn the rules
Pretty obvious, right?
Well, some people play dozens of chess games before fully understanding how castling works and what the en passant move is, so don't be like them and learn the rules from the beginning. Here is a checklist:
  • how pieces move
  • castling
  • pawn promotion
  • en passant
  • check
  • checkmate
  • stalemate
  • the 50 moves rule
  • draw by insufficient material
Learn elementary checkmates
Alright, now you know that winning a game means checkmating your opponent so you have to know how to checkmate your opponent. There are a lot of ways to do so but some are fundamental and will often occur during your games:
  • the ladder mate
  • the back-rank mate
  • any mate that consists in a queen stuck to the opponent's king with a piece defending the queen
Learn the values of the pieces
Now, it is important to understand that all pieces at chess are not worth the same. If you already played some games, you already noticed that a queen is much more dangerous than a pawn or a bishop. So when it comes to exchanging pieces, you always have to be aware about the value of the pieces that are being exchanged so that the exchange is not negative for you (well it can be if it is a sacrifice). Here are the values of the pieces:
  • Pawn: 1
  • Knight: 3
  • Bishop: 3
  • Rook: 5
  • Queen: 9
Note that the king does not have any point because losing it means losing the game.
Learn the principles of the opening
Quite often, when you start playing chess, you have no idea about what to play at the beginning of a game. Actually, even at the beginning, some moves are better than others so it is important that you know the principles of the opening so you kind of have an idea about what to play at the beginning of a game. So remember, during the opening, you should:
  • Control the center of the board with your pieces
  • Develop your pieces (which means you have to take your bishops and knights out from their initial square so that they enter in the game to control more strategic squares)
  • Castle (remember that in general, castling long is a bit more unsafe for your king than castling short)
  • Not take your queen out too early. Indeed, in the previous step, you saw that it is your most powerful piece so it would be a pity to loose it because of a trap by your opponent during the opening.
  • Not move the same piece twice in a row (because it makes you "loose" time meanwhile you could develop another piece that was not developed yet)
  • Connect your rooks. That menas having your rooks "looking at each other" because you took out your bishops and your knight, because you castled and because you took your queen out (but not too early)

Then, each piece has a sign:
  • Pawn: no sign
  • Bishop: B
  • Knight: C
  • Rook: R
  • Queen: Q
  • King: K
So, Cf4 means that a knight is going on the f3 square and c4 means that a pawn is going on the c4 square. Then, to say that a piece is taking another piece, you write an "x". So that, Cxf7 means that a Knight is taking a piece on the f7 square. When a pawn takes another piece, you just write its column, so that dxc4 means that a pawn on the d column takes a piece on the c4 square. When there is check, we write a "+"sign and when there is checkmate, we write a "#" sign.
Learn elementary tactics
At this moment of your journey, you have to learn some basic tactics that often occur during a chess game. Here are the most common:
  • The forks: this is when you attack two pieces simultaneously. You will see in your games that you generally fork with knights.
  • The skewers: this when you attack to pieces that are on the same row, column or diagonal.
Learn 5 checkmates in the opening
You may use them against low-rated players. Here is my selection but there are more:
  • The Fool's mate: 1. f3 e6 2. g4 Qh4#
  • The Scholar's mate: 1. e4 e5 2. Bf4 Bc5 3. Qh5 Cc6 4. Qxf7#
  • The Caro Kann's smothered mate: 1. e4 c6 2. d4 d5 3. Cc3 dxe4 4. Cxe4 Cd7 5. Qe2 Cgf6 6. Cd6#
  • The quick Englund Gambit mate: 1. d4 e5 2. dxe5 Qe7 3. Cf3 Cc6 4. Bf4 Qb4+ 5. Bd2 Qxb2 6. Bc3 Bb4 7. Qd2 Bxc3 8. Qxc3 Qc1#
  • The quick Owen defense mate: 1. e4 b6 2. d4 Bb7 3. Bd3 f5 4. exf5 Bxg2 5. Qh5+ g6 6. fxg6 Cf6 7. gxh7 Cxh5 8. Bg6#
